# Hook Pattern Library

## Category 1: Curiosity Gap

Creates an information gap the reader must close.

**Patterns:**
- "[Result] — and [unexpected detail]."
- "The [thing] nobody talks about when [doing X]:"
- "I [did thing] for [timeframe]. Here's what actually happened:"
- "[Common activity] has a hidden [cost/benefit] most people miss."
- "There's a reason [known thing] works — and it's not what you think."

**Why it works:** Phoenix's dwell signal spikes when users pause to process an incomplete mental model. The gap creates cognitive tension that demands resolution.

**Risk:** Low, as long as the content delivers on the curiosity.

---

## Category 2: Specific Number

Concrete metrics stop the scroll because they're immediately verifiable and remarkable.

**Patterns:**
- "[Exact number] [result] in [timeframe]."
- "[X]× [improvement] with [single change]."
- "From [bad number] to [good number] in [timeframe]."
- "[N] [things] that [specific outcome]. [Tease one]."
- "Spent [time/money] on [thing]. ROI: [specific result]."

**Why it works:** Numbers are processed faster than text by the brain. Specific numbers (2.3× not "2×", 847 not "hundreds") feel real and trustworthy.

**Risk:** Very low. Numbers rarely trigger negative signals unless they seem fraudulent.

---

## Category 3: Contrarian

Challenges what the audience believes. High ceiling, moderate risk.

**Patterns:**
- "Unpopular opinion: [common practice] is [negative outcome]."
- "Stop [common advice]. It's [actually harmful/useless]."
- "Everyone says [do X]. Here's why I [do opposite]:"
- "[Respected thing] is overrated. Here's what I use instead:"
- "The [industry] is lying about [topic]. Here's the data:"

**Why it works:** Triggers an immediate emotional response — agreement or disagreement. Both lead to dwell time (processing the claim) and replies (defending position).

**Risk:** Moderate. Poorly calibrated contrarian takes trigger "not interested" from people who feel attacked. The take must be defensible.

---

## Category 4: Pain Point

Names a frustration your audience experiences. Creates instant identification.

**Patterns:**
- "[Audience] are still [frustrating thing] in [current year]."
- "Tired of [common pain point]? [Brief tease of solution]."
- "Why does [common thing] still [frustrating behavior]?"
- "The [tool/process] tax is real: [specific cost in time/money/sanity]."
- "[Activity] shouldn't take [ridiculous amount of time/effort]."

**Why it works:** Identification is instant — "this person understands my problem." Creates trust and pulls the reader into the solution.

**Risk:** Low. Pain point hooks rarely trigger negatives because they resonate.

---

## Category 5: Before/After

Shows transformation in minimal words. The gap between states creates desire.

**Patterns:**
- "[Before state] → [After state]."
- "[Old way]: [pain]. [New way]: [relief]."
- "Last [time period]: [bad state]. Today: [good state]. What changed:"
- "[Metric before] to [metric after] in [timeframe]."
- "My [thing] [time ago] vs now:"

**Why it works:** The brain immediately wants to understand the transformation mechanism. Creates both dwell (reading to understand) and bookmark (saving the method).

**Risk:** Very low.

---

## Category 6: Social Proof

Leads with credibility markers. Works best when the proof is genuinely impressive.

**Patterns:**
- "[Impressive metric] and counting."
- "[N] [people/companies] are now using [thing]."
- "[Authority/source] just [validated your thing]."
- "After [experience duration], here's what I've learned:"
- "[Impressive result] — here's exactly what we did:"

**Why it works:** Social proof reduces skepticism and signals the content is worth reading. If many people found value, the reader expects to as well.

**Risk:** Low, unless the social proof feels inflated or the content doesn't match the credibility claim.

---

## Category 7: Question

Poses something the reader can't scroll past without thinking about.

**Patterns:**
- "What's the [most X] [thing] you've ever [done/seen]?"
- "Why does nobody talk about [overlooked topic]?"
- "If you could only [constraint], what would you [choose]?"
- "What would you [do] if [hypothetical with real implications]?"
- "Am I the only one who [common but unspoken experience]?"

**Why it works:** Questions activate the brain's response mechanism. People instinctively formulate an answer, which increases dwell time and reply probability.

**Risk:** Low, but weak questions ("thoughts?", "agree?") feel lazy and don't trigger the response mechanism.

---

## Category 8: Urgency/Timeliness

Ties to something happening now. Creates FOMO or relevance pressure.

**Patterns:**
- "[Just happened]: [implication for audience]."
- "[Tool/platform] just [changed something]. Here's what it means:"
- "If you're still [old way] after [recent event], you're [missing out]."
- "[New thing] changes everything about [audience's workflow]."

**Why it works:** Timeliness creates urgency. The reader feels they'll miss out if they don't read now.

**Risk:** Low-moderate. Artificial urgency ("BREAKING" on non-breaking news) backfires.

---

## Hook Combination Techniques

The strongest hooks combine 2 patterns:

- **Number + Curiosity**: "2.3× faster — and I only changed one line."
- **Pain + Contrarian**: "Your test suite is lying to you. Here's proof:"
- **Social Proof + Before/After**: "10K stars later, here's what I'd do differently from day 1."
- **Question + Pain**: "Why are we still writing boilerplate in 2026?"
